Your job is not competing with the Great Commission. It is one of the main places you can live it.

You spend more waking hours with your coworkers than with almost anyone else in your life. You know their moods, their deadlines, and the family news that leaks into a Monday morning. They know yours. For many working Christians, that is the largest and steadiest group of people who do not yet follow Jesus that they will ever have real access to.

The barrier is rarely desire. It is that disciple-making usually gets described as one more thing to add on top of a full life: a program to join, an evening to give up, a role to earn. So the job becomes the thing you get through until the real ministry starts somewhere else, on some other night, with some other group of people.

This guide takes the opposite approach. It assumes you already have work, a family, and a limited calendar, and it walks through how to make disciples inside that life instead of alongside it. For the broader biblical pattern underneath all of this, Multiplying Disciples has a fuller guide on how to make disciples. Here the focus stays narrow: your coworkers, your ordinary week, and the next honest step you can actually take.

Your Workplace Is Already a Mission Field

Key idea

Your job is not the obstacle to making disciples. For most people, it is the primary location of it.

Think about what a job actually gives you. You are alongside the same people day after day, through deadlines, stress, small wins, and ordinary conversations. That kind of proximity, built slowly over months and years, is something no outreach event can manufacture. A paid minister does not have access to your coworkers. You do, and not because you are more gifted or better trained. You are simply there, every day, in the same building or on the same team.

Making disciples at work does not start with a bold plan. It starts with becoming spiritually available in the middle of a normal shift: paying attention to who is carrying something heavy, listening when someone actually answers the question, and being willing to talk about what is real when the moment comes. Most of your week is already spent among people far from God. The question is whether you are awake to it.

Start With People You Already Know

You do not have to go find a mission field. You are standing in one. The people already in your life, your family, coworkers, neighbors, clients, and teammates, are where disciple-making almost always begins.

There is an old word for this relational world: oikos, the household and network of people you already know. You do not need the word to use the idea. Most people have somewhere between eight and fifteen of these relationships active at any time. The first move is to name them.

Write down the actual names of five to ten coworkers or regular contacts who do not yet follow Jesus or seem spiritually open. Not "my team" or "the people at work," but names. Then pray for each one by name this week. Naming people in prayer changes how you see them; once you have prayed for someone by name, you start noticing openings you used to miss.

As you pray, watch for openness without treating anyone as a project. People can tell the difference between someone who cares about them and someone who is running them through a process. Look for the coworker who lingers after a hard meeting, who mentions a struggle at home, who asks a question that goes a little deeper than usual. That is often where God is already at work.

Move Conversations Toward Jesus

If you have stayed quiet at work, it probably is not for lack of conviction. It is that moving a conversation from small talk to something real is a skill most of us were never taught.

It helps to picture conversations moving through four spaces: casual, meaningful, spiritual, and discovery. Each step is just a slightly more honest question than the last.

  • Casual to meaningful: "How are you, really?" or "How did that thing with your son turn out?" You are showing that you remember and that you actually want the true answer.
  • Meaningful to spiritual: "That sounds heavy. How are you holding up underneath it all?" or "Has faith ever been part of your life?"
  • Spiritual to discovery: "Would you want to read a short story about Jesus with me sometime and talk about it?"

The point is not to run a script or climb all four stairs in one lunch break. Listening and genuine care come first, every time. You are paying attention to the person in front of you and taking one honest step when it is there, then letting it rest. For the full framework, with specific language for each transition, see how to start spiritual conversations naturally.

Share the Gospel Clearly and Appropriately

Key idea

Kindness opens the door. It is not the same as the gospel. At some point the good news has to be spoken.

You can be the most decent person on the team and never actually tell anyone why. Being ethical, calm under pressure, and generous with your time matters, and it earns trust, but no one is changed by your character alone. Somewhere in the relationship, the actual message about Jesus has to be said out loud.

Two simple tools carry most of the weight. The first is your own story: what your life was like, how you met Jesus, and what is different now. Putting a short version into words ahead of time makes it easy to share when a door opens. The second is a simple gospel explanation. You do not need to invent your own outline. Something like the 3 Circles walks a person from the brokenness we all feel to the good news of Jesus and a real response, in a few lines you can draw on a napkin.

When you share, give the person a real chance to respond. A few plain questions do the work: "Does this make sense?" or "Is anything keeping you from following Jesus?" You are not manufacturing pressure, just offering an honest opening. Jesus tied response and baptism together from the start: "Go and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, and teaching them to obey everything I have commanded you" (Matthew 28:19-20).

Work adds real boundaries, and honoring them is part of doing this well. Respect your employer's policies. Be careful with power differences, and never lean on someone who reports to you, a client who depends on you, a student, a patient, or anyone in a vulnerable position. When a conversation needs more room than a shift allows, move it to a break, a meal, or off-hours. Boundaries like these keep your witness honest rather than weakening it.

Move From Workplace Contact to Shared Life

Work is often where a relationship begins, but it is rarely where discipleship goes deepest. The desk, the shift, and the shared project can only hold so much. Reading Scripture together, praying regularly, and being honest about real change usually need a different setting: a meal, your home, a park bench, a coffee shop, a commute, a video call, or another third space away from the job.

This is what the early church called being with one another. Disciples are formed less in scheduled Bible lessons than in shared life: over meals, in the middle of ordinary problems, while serving someone, and while trying to obey a hard command for the first time. Jesus called the Twelve to be with him before he ever sent them out.

So invite people into your actual life. Make room for prayer, a short passage of Scripture, a shared meal, serving someone together, practicing how to share the gospel, working through a real problem, and honest follow-up on what each of you said you would do. Discipleship happens through shared obedience, not only through information handed across a table.

Train Through MAWL

Key idea

If it only works when you are in the room, you have built a following, not a disciple who can go on without you.

Nothing in this guide can be handed over by explanation alone. It has to be trained, and the simplest pattern for that is MAWL:

  1. Model: you do it while they watch.
  2. Assist: you do it together, and they help.
  3. Watch: they take the lead while you watch and give feedback.
  4. Launch: they do it on their own, and start training someone else.

Run anything through that pattern: praying out loud, sharing a testimony, walking someone through the gospel, reading Scripture together, leading a simple discipleship conversation, or gathering a few people. Do not just tell a new believer how to pray for their coworkers by name. Pray with them while they watch, then while they help, then step back and let them lead, then send them to do it with someone you will never meet. The goal from the first day is confident obedience that does not depend on you.

Gather New Disciples With Other Believers

Do not leave a new disciple on their own. Jesus never told us to make isolated followers. A new believer needs other believers to obey Jesus alongside, people who will pray with them, correct them gently, and keep them going.

Two early steps matter here, and neither needs to be complicated. Baptism belongs near the beginning of the journey, not at the end; in Acts, people were baptized the same day they believed. And a new disciple needs a church, which is simply baptized believers who gather around Jesus to obey him together, share the Lord's Supper, pray, care for one another, and keep reaching others. The workplace may open the relationship, but the whole life of Jesus is practiced best in a setting built for it. If a group starts forming out of your workplace relationships, a simple multiplying church shows what that can look like without a building or a program.

Notice and Develop Emerging Leaders

As disciples obey, some begin to lead, and it usually looks less dramatic than you would expect. Leadership here is not mainly charisma, title, gifting, or platform. It is stewardship: someone taking responsibility for following Jesus and helping others do the same.

Watch for the people who:

  • Obey what Jesus says, even when it is inconvenient.
  • Take responsibility instead of waiting to be told.
  • Genuinely care for the people around them.
  • Gather others rather than only attending.
  • Share what they are learning with someone else.
  • Keep going when it gets hard.
  • Help other people take real steps.

When you see those marks, your job shifts. You are no longer keeping someone dependent on you. You are helping them steward the people and place God has given them, the way someone once did for you. A person of peace in their own department or friend group is often the doorway into a network you could never reach on your own.

Build Toward Multiplication

From the first week, the aim is for the person you disciple to disciple someone else. Paul put it plainly when he trained Timothy:

"And the things you have heard me say in the presence of many witnesses entrust to reliable people who will also be qualified to teach others." (2 Timothy 2:2)

Count the generations in one verse: Paul, to Timothy, to reliable people, to others. That is the pattern to keep in view. Help each disciple name the people they can reach, the coworkers and friends and family already in their own world. Train them to repeat what you did with them, in language plain enough to pass on again. The result is more people carrying the same small, reproducible obedience, so ordinary believers make disciples, disciples become leaders, and simple churches begin to start other churches.

A Simple Week to Begin

You do not need a strategy retreat. You need seven days and a few honest steps that fit around a normal workweek.

  • Monday: Write down five coworkers or regular contacts who do not yet follow Jesus.
  • Tuesday: Pray for each of them by name before you start your day.
  • Wednesday: Move one conversation from casual to meaningful by asking a real follow-up question.
  • Thursday: Offer to pray for someone who is going through something, briefly and without making a scene.
  • Friday: Share a short version of your own story with one person when it fits.
  • Saturday: Invite someone into your life outside of work: a meal, a coffee, or a short Scripture over text.
  • Sunday: Ask, "What is God asking me to obey next?" and plan the one step.

Then repeat it. Most people overestimate what it takes to begin and underestimate what a week of small, repeated obedience will do.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I make disciples at work without violating professional boundaries?

Yes. Making disciples at work is not about pushing religion on people or using work time for a personal agenda. It means doing excellent work, caring genuinely, and being honest about your faith when a real conversation opens. Respect your employer's policies, keep it to breaks or off-hours when needed, and never lean on someone who reports to you or depends on you. Good boundaries make your witness more credible, not less.

How do I start a spiritual conversation with a coworker?

Start by listening. Move a normal conversation one honest step deeper: ask how someone is really doing, follow up on something hard they mentioned, or share a small piece of your own story. When trust is there, a simple question like "Has faith ever been part of your life?" opens the door without forcing it.

What if my workplace discourages religious conversations?

Honor the policy. Plenty of disciple-making happens outside the building: a lunch off-site, a coffee after work, a text on the weekend. You can still pray for coworkers by name, do your job with integrity, and offer to pray for someone privately when they are going through something. When a relationship is ready for more, move it to your own time and space.

When should workplace discipleship move outside of work?

When the relationship outgrows the setting. Work is a good place to begin, but reading Scripture together, praying regularly, and sharing life usually need a table, a home, a park, or a video call. As soon as someone is genuinely open, invite them into a space where you can go deeper without the clock or the job in the way.

Do I need formal training to make disciples at work?

No. You need to be following Jesus yourself and willing to share what you are learning. Training and coaching help you get more competent, and CoVo exists to give you that practice, but the qualification to start is obedience, not a certificate.

How do I know when someone is ready to disciple others?

Look at obedience, not knowledge. Someone is ready when they are following Jesus in the ordinary parts of their week, caring for people around them, and already telling others what they are learning. You do not wait until they feel ready. You give them something small to do, do it with them, and step back as they grow.

Your Next Step

Making disciples at work was never meant to require a program, a platform, or a certain personality. It takes obedience, a few real relationships, and a willingness to start smaller than you think you should.
