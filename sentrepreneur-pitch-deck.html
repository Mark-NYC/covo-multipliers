Model

Copy Block
Launchpad is the first-stage pilot cohort inside Sentrepreneur Academy.
Pilot Focus
  • 5–10 emerging leaders per cohort
  • 6–12 month program duration

  • Weekly coaching and real-world reps

Horizontal timeline: Pre-cohort → Month 1–2 (Training + Basics) → Month 3–6 (Reps + Coaching) → Month 6–12 (Launch + Documentation) → Post-cohort (Case Study + Next Stage). Simple horizontal bar or milestone line. Muted gold milestone dots.

Design Notes
Timeline runs horizontally across the bottom third of the slide. Bullet list above-left. Copy block above-right or inline. Timeline dots in muted gold. Phase labels in charcoal. Keep the timeline clean — 4 to 5 phases max.

Why This Is Different
Model
Main Headline
Not business school. Not ministry training. Not startup hype.
Comparison Table
Business School
Often teaches concepts without ownership. No real customers, no coaching, no mission integration.
Ministry Training
Often teaches mission apart from work. Disciple-making happens in a room, not a real-life context.
Startup Hype
Often chases ideas without discipline. Big vision, no reps, no accountability, no formation.
Sentrepreneur Academy
Trains builders through real work, real coaching, real disciple-making, and staged responsibility.
Visual Concept
Four-column comparison table. First three columns in light neutral with charcoal text (representing alternatives). Fourth column in dark green with white text (Sentrepreneur Academy). Each column has a bold label at top and a two-sentence descriptor below. No checkmarks or X marks — just clear language.
Speaker Note
The differentiation has to be brutally clear. We are combining what usually stays separated — business formation and mission integration, ownership and coaching, practical work and disciple-making. That combination is the product.
Design Notes
Avoid a traditional feature-comparison table with checkmarks. Instead, use a four-column card layout. The Academy column stands visually apart through color, not just a checkmark column. The contrast should feel decisive, not defensive.

Investment Thesis
Investor Case
Main Headline
Fund the process, not just the person.
Copy Block
One-off funding for young entrepreneurs is risky. Launchpad reduces risk through a structured process.
How the Process Reduces Risk
  • Selection — not everyone gets in
  • Staged training before funding increases
  • Weekly coaching and accountability
  • Low-overhead business starts limit early burn
  • Clear ownership expectations from day one
  • Measurable milestones at each stage gate
  • Community accountability through the hub and cohort
Visual Concept
Risk funnel diagram. Wide at top: "Many interested." Narrowing: "Fewer selected." Narrower: "Trained and coached." Narrow: "Launched with support." Narrowest: "Funded at scale." The funnel shape communicates that the process filters for readiness, not just enthusiasm. Labels at each funnel stage.
Speaker Note
This is the investor logic. The process is the asset — not any individual participant. The process can be repeated, improved, and scaled. That is what investors are actually funding.
Design Notes
Funnel graphic right side of slide. Bullet list left side. Keep the funnel visually clean — no clip art funnels. Use layered trapezoid shapes in progressively darker shades of green. Label each layer with text to the right of the funnel.

Risk and Mitigation
Investor Case
Main Headline
The risk is real. That is why the process matters.
Risk / Mitigation Grid
Risk
Not everyone who applies is ready to build.
Mitigation
Staged selection and entry requirements before funding begins.
Risk
Young builders can chase the next idea instead of executing.
Mitigation
Weekly coaching, defined milestones, and small starts that require focus.
Risk
Unclear financial arrangements create conflict.
Mitigation
Clear terms written before funds are released. No ambiguity.
Risk
Mission and business goals can drift apart or create confusion.
Mitigation
Integrated coaching holds both together. Metrics track both business and mission outcomes.
Visual Concept
Two-column grid. Left column header "Risk" in charcoal. Right column header "Mitigation" in dark green. Four rows. Left cells warm neutral. Right cells light green. Alternating rows subtle — not striped — just distinct enough to track horizontally. Keep font small enough to fit cleanly.
Speaker Note
Do not hide risk. Name it and show how the pathway is designed to handle it. Investors who have funded people before have seen every one of these risks. Showing them you have thought it through builds trust faster than minimizing the risks.
Design Notes
Keep the table clean and readable at presentation distance. Risk column slightly indented. Mitigation column in a lighter background to signal "resolved." Headline at top. No need for other body copy — the table is the content.

  • Risk rows (add, remove, or reword as the model evolves)

15
Ownership Philosophy
Investor Case
Main Headline
We do not build businesses for people.
Core Conviction
The founder must come in saying: "I want to do this. Help me build it." Ownership has to be present from the beginning — it cannot be handed to someone later and expected to stick.
What This Means Practically
  • Participants pursue the opportunity, not the other way around
  • The work belongs to the builder, not the academy
  • Coaching supports ownership — it does not replace it
  • Funding comes alongside initiative, not instead of it
Visual Concept
A single person holding a steering wheel, a set of keys, or the reins of something — a clear visual metaphor for ownership and agency. Simple, bold, not overly literal. Could be illustrated or a clean photograph. Dark background optional for this slide to give it weight.
Speaker Note
This is a core conviction, not just a program policy. You cannot hand someone ownership later and hope the DNA sticks. It has to start in their posture from day one. We look for it during selection, and we reinforce it throughout the program.
Design Notes
This slide can afford a slightly bolder visual treatment — larger image, minimal text. The headline is the strongest copy line in the deck. Let it breathe. Bullet list small and to one side. Image takes the dominant visual real estate.

  • "What this means practically" bullet list

What Funding Supports
Investor Case
Main Headline
What investor dollars make possible.
Funding Buckets
  • Cohort training and facilitation
  • Coaching fees and mentor compensation
  • Curriculum development and tools
  • Apprenticeship support and placement
  • Startup kits or micro-grants for participants
  • Legal and accounting setup assistance
  • Reporting, documentation, and case study production
  • Hub and immersion infrastructure
Visual Concept
Budget allocation graphic. Horizontal bar chart or donut chart with each funding bucket as a labeled segment. Percentages should be editable placeholders. Color coding: dark green for largest allocation, gold for secondary, charcoal for operational items. Exact percentages to be filled in based on actual budget.
Speaker Note
Make the money concrete. Investors need to see where it goes. Vague use-of-funds slides signal unclear planning. If percentages change before the pitch, update this slide — it matters more than most.
Design Notes
Use a horizontal stacked bar or a simple donut chart — not a pie chart. Bar chart is easier to read at distance. Each segment labeled directly (not in a legend). Percentages as editable text. Headline top-left. Chart takes bottom 50% of slide.
